Fun & Curious Papers

A shelf of writing I enjoyed making — some decode brains, some decode markets, some stare at medical images, one or two wonder aloud what it means to live next to a machine that can almost do everything.
Xupeng Chen · author's shelf Twelve entries · eleven published · one essay

These are not reading lists; they are stories. I wrote them on trains, in small cafés, and one of them on the floor of a hospital waiting room. If you're curious enough to click one, you already deserve the punchline. Each card below opens into a proper write-up — with figures, numbers, interactive toys and the occasional confession — not just an abstract.

The pieces are grouped into three shelves. Brains & speech is where I spend most of my time; six papers on decoding the cortex. AI & the world around it is the shelf where I'm a tourist, armed with a pen; three essays on what happens when thinking becomes cheap. Vision & the edges is the miscellaneous shelf — medical images, drones, pixels. You can read them in any order.

Distributed feedforward & feedback cortical processing supports human speech production.

Is speech production serial or parallel? We built a causal analysis across frontal and temporal cortex and found both — feedforward motor drive and feedback from auditory regions, happening at the same time in overlapping windows. A small blow to textbook schematics, a small gift to the BCI community.

Subject-agnostic transformer-based neural speech decoding, from both surface and depth electrodes.

Most decoders memorise the subject they were trained on. We built a transformer that generalises across people — ECoG grids, sEEG depth probes, different implants, different regions — without retraining. The same model listens to any cortex you put in front of it. One of the projects I am quietly the proudest of.

VoxelFormer, parameter-efficient visual decoding from many brains at once.

Reconstructing what someone is seeing from their fMRI usually means one model per subject and an enormous adapter. VoxelFormer asks: what if the voxels themselves are the tokens and the model is one, shared, tiny transformer? Competitive retrieval, a fraction of the parameters, many subjects at once.

Predicting speech arrest during cortical stimulation, before anyone touches the scalpel.

Before epilepsy surgery, neurologists stimulate individual electrodes to find the ones that must not be resected — the ones whose stimulation freezes the patient mid-word. It's slow, painful, and sometimes you run out of time. We built an ML model that predicts which electrodes will cause arrest from resting-state data alone.
